For my thesis, I will attempt to improve the integration of weather station data with remote sensing estimates of snow water equivalent (SWE). Passive microwave remote sensing is suitable for global, synoptic SWE estimation [1], but becomes less accurate with high SWE values and over complex land cover types [2,3]. To better constrain uncertainty in SWE estimates, passive microwave imagery can be combined with in situ observations [4]. The method I will be using is based on the European Space Agency’s GlobSnow project [5]. By reducing the uncertainty in such a product, uncertainty in the climate data record could be reduced.

My research will focus on the spatial interpolation of the in situ data aspect of the SWE estimation process. The GlobSnow method uses Ordinary Kriging to interpolate the discrete weather observations, which assumes a constant mean across the entire domain. As that domain covers entire continents, it is unlike that snowpack characteristics are constant across such a large area and Ordinary Kriging may not be the optimal approach. Therefore, I hope to identify a better approach to interpolate weather observations and reduce uncertainty in the final SWE estimates.

A reduced uncertainty in trends of snowpack characteristics aligns well with the United Nation’s Sustainable Development Goal 13 - Take urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts . In order to properly address climate change, it first must be properly understood and quantified. Snowpack characteristics are an important aspect of characterising climate change, namely SWE, and are used in climate, hydrology, and energy modelling.
